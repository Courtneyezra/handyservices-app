# Handyman Pay Models — Deep Research & Verdict

_Nottingham / HandyServices · 21 Jul 2026 · derived from a fan-out web-research run (91 claims extracted, 75 adversarially verified). Refuted sub-claims were dropped; corrections noted inline._

## TL;DR

Your three instincts are **not equally hard**. Ranked by how binding they actually are:

1. **"Want consistent work"** → **REAL need, closest to a hard constraint.** This is your single biggest recruiting asset, because you own demand and most self-employed handymen can't get a full book. Lean on it hard.
2. **"Prefer performance pay / day rate is insulting"** → **Soft & partly a myth.** It's segment-dependent, and the market has already moved past the binary to **hybrid (floor + performance)**. Reframable.
3. **"Don't want to quote/invoice"** → **Soft & actually a strategic gift** — you already quote via the AI engine. But removing quoting has a *legal* price (see below).

**The real binding constraint is none of the three — it's UK employment-status law.** Every instinct you want to satisfy (remove quoting, control quality, pay a day rate) pushes a worker toward "worker"/employee status. That is the constraint to design around.

---

## Instinct 1 — "Handymen prefer performance pay; day rate is insulting / caps earnings"

**Verdict: SOFT / REFRAMABLE, and partly a myth.**

What the evidence actually says:

- **Preference is segment-dependent, not universal.** High-output, proactive tradespeople prefer flat/performance pay (fast techs can earn ~double an hourly wage by being efficient — verified). Security-minded and younger workers prefer stable pay ("predictability matters more than upside" — verified). Treat "performance-preferred" as a *disposition*, not a fact about all handymen.
- **The market has moved to hybrid.** The ASE/WrenchWay 2026 *Voice of the Technician* survey (~5,500 respondents) found preference for **hourly/salary + production bonus jumped from ~25% to ~41–43% in one year — now the single most-preferred model**, while straight flat-rate is a minority. Income unpredictability under pure flat-rate is "one of the most consistent reasons techs leave." (US auto-mechanic data, used as analog — directionally solid, medium confidence.)
- **Pure-performance handyman work in the wild has poor retention.** Aspect Maintenance (London) pays uncapped commission — reviewers cite £6–10k/month — **but** carries a 2.5/5 Indeed rating (51 reviews), constant pay disputes, unpaid driving-time between jobs, and perverse pressure to over-charge customers. Uncapped upside ≠ happy, retained workforce.
- **"Day rate is insulting" — no evidence found for the emotion; there IS evidence for the mechanism.** Day rate removes the effort/upside link. That's a real complaint, but it's a *design* problem you fix by adding upside, not an identity slight.

⚠️ **Correction (verifier-refuted):** the common claim that "day rate incentivises finishing efficiently" is **false and backwards** — a day-rate worker has every incentive to *stretch* the job. It's **price/piece work** that rewards speed. This matters for your model choice.

**So:** don't offer a naked day rate, and don't over-rotate to naked commission either. Offer **piece/price-per-job (uncapped) with a predictable floor.** That's the hybrid the market now wants.

## Instinct 2 — "Handymen want consistent, steady work"

**Verdict: REAL retention driver — the closest thing to a hard constraint, and your biggest lever.**

- **Schedule volatility (irregular hours, last-minute shifts) is a primary reason tradespeople quit** (verified). Income volatility is the core structural drawback of self-employment (IFS/ONS-corroborated).
- **Franchises sell exactly this.** Jim's Group and Hire A Hubby both headline income guarantees (HAH: £25k–£50k+ gross-profit guarantee to new franchisees) precisely because steady income is the thing self-employed trades can't get themselves.
  - ⚠️ **Correction:** Jim's "$1,200/week guarantee" is **not** a passive cash floor — the verifiers found it's a "pay-for-work" scheme (do free jobs, get reimbursed ~$90/job) and the figure varies $1,000–2,500 by division. Don't copy it as a simple salary floor.
- Tradespeople judge the **whole package** (rate + benefits + tool allowance + progression), not headline pay (verified). Opaque/uncompetitive packages cause attrition.

**Strategic point:** You run a demand engine. **A full book of pre-sold, pre-priced work is a benefit no independent handyman can buy.** This is your recruiting pitch — "we keep you busy" beats "we pay you X." The 1↔2 tension ("performance vs stability") dissolves the moment *you* supply the volume: more available pre-priced jobs = more earnings AND more predictability at once.

## Instinct 3 — "Handymen don't want to quote and invoice"

**Verdict: SOFT / REFRAMABLE — and a gift you're uniquely placed to give — but it carries a legal cost.**

- The UK subcontractor split maps this exactly:
  - **Labour-only subcontractor (LOSC):** paid hourly/daily, works under *your* instruction with *your* tools, **doesn't quote.** → But is treated as **your employee for employers'-liability insurance**, and **you absorb the delivery/quality risk** (no work-guarantee from an LOSC).
  - **Bona-fide subcontractor (BFSC):** **quotes the job upfront**, paid on completion, carries own insurance, fixes defects at own cost, works to own schedule.
- **You've already automated the quote.** Because your AI engine produces the price, you can remove *quote authorship* from the handyman without making him an LOSC — he **accepts a pre-priced job and invoices for it**. He never writes a quote; he still invoices (which preserves self-employment).
- ⚠️ **Correction:** franchise "job management systems" (Hire A Hubby) don't remove invoicing — they give franchisees *tools* to invoice faster. Nobody in the market fully removes invoicing from a genuinely self-employed trade, because invoicing is part of what *makes* them self-employed.

**So:** you can kill quoting (your moat), but keep **invoicing** on the handyman's side — it's cheap for him and legally load-bearing for you.

## The actual hard constraint: UK employment-status law

This is what should drive the design, more than any instinct. Heavily verified against ACAS, GOV.UK, HMRC ESM, and case law.

- **Status follows reality, not paperwork.** *Pimlico Plumbers v Smith* [2018] UKSC 29: a plumber contractually "self-employed," VAT-registered, taxed as self-employed, was held to be a **"worker"** (→ holiday pay, etc.). Facts that sank it: **minimum ~40-hr/week obligation, no genuine right of substitution, branded van + uniform, all customer contact routed through the company.**
- **Three tests:** (1) **substitution** — a genuine, unfettered right to send a substitute points to self-employment; (2) **control** — who decides how/when/where; (3) **mutuality of obligation.**
- **The three "reclassification red flags" you are walking toward:** paying a **day rate**, **controlling how the work is done**, and **providing tools** — plus removing quoting and setting hours. Stack enough of these and you've built an employee, with holiday pay, NI, pension auto-enrolment, tribunal exposure, and employers'-liability liability.
- ⚠️ **Correction:** don't lean on CIS registration as proof of self-employment. A first-instance tribunal (Vitoroi) that reached that conclusion did so only because status was *conceded*; the binding authority (Pimlico) says a CIS/self-employed *label* does not decide status.

**Keeping a handyman genuinely self-employed (design levers):**
- Genuine right of substitution (a real one, exercised in practice — not a paper clause).
- Job/deliverable framing, not rotas, set hours, or daily reporting.
- His own tools; his own public-liability insurance; his own risk assessment / method statement.
- He **invoices** you (per job); you don't issue payslips.
- You set **service levels and acceptance criteria** (the outcome), not **work methods** (how he does it).

**The trap to avoid:** control like an employee, pay like a contractor. That's the Pimlico fact pattern. If you genuinely need to control method, uniform, schedule and reliability (your "reliability-per-promise" goal for Core), then **employ those people properly (PAYE)** and stop pretending — it's cheaper than a reclassification finding.

## Recommended pay-model design

**Core principle: price-per-job (piece rate) on YOUR prices, with a predictable floor, kept genuinely self-employed via substitution + own tools + invoicing.** This threads all three instincts at once:

| Instinct | How the model satisfies it |
|---|---|
| 1. Performance / no insulting day rate | **Piece rate per job** — uncapped, more jobs = more money, no day-rate cap |
| 2. Consistency | **You supply the book** of pre-priced work + optional weekly volume commitment / floor |
| 3. No quoting | **Your AI engine sets the price;** he accepts pre-priced jobs (still invoices — legally load-bearing) |

Mapped onto your 3-tier delivery OS:

- **Ad-hoc (ship first).** Pure price-per-job. Genuinely self-employed BFSC: CIS, own tools/insurance, genuine substitution, invoices per job, no guaranteed volume. Lowest legal risk, fastest to recruit. *This is your MVP and it's already the direction of your ad-hoc agreement work.*

- **Core (design carefully — pick a lane).** You want reliability-per-promise here, which means control, which fights self-employment. Two honest options:
  - **(a) Priority subcontractor + volume commitment:** piece-rate + a *commercial* commitment to offer N jobs/£X of pre-priced work per week (their income floor), **but no control over method/hours** and a real substitution right. Stays self-employed. Softer reliability.
  - **(b) Genuine employment (PAYE):** if you truly need to dictate uniform, schedule, quality process and exclusivity — **employ them.** Salary or day-rate-as-employee + performance bonus. Highest reliability, highest cost/commitment. This is the legitimate home for "day rate," because as an *employee* a day rate isn't a misclassification risk.
  - Recommendation: start Core as **(a)**, graduate proven techs to **(b)** as volume justifies fixed cost.

- **Partner.** Revenue-share / referral (post-job upsell, retention) — leave as-is.

**Numbers for Nottingham (verified ranges):** UK handyman £30–50/hr, day rate £180–280/8hr; North/Midlands runs **20–35% below London**; headline charge ≠ take-home (a £60/hr charge nets ~£25–35/hr after overheads). Price your per-job rates so a good handyman clears a genuinely competitive **effective** hourly, and make the floor real enough to matter.

**The pitch that recruits (from the evidence):** not "we pay the most" — it's **"we bring the work and the price; you bring the tools and the quality; you never chase a quote or a lead again, and we keep you busy."** That sells consistency (instinct 2) + no-admin (instinct 3) + uncapped piece-rate upside (instinct 1) in one line — and it's exactly what a demand-owning company can credibly promise.
